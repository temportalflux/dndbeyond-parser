Description

One of the many predators to stalk the marshlands and muddy foothills of Xhorhas, moorbounders are known as dangerous killers to those who try to avoid them on their travels‚Äîand as prized mounts for those willing to capture and train them. Though the process of domesticating a moorbounder is fraught with peril, these creatures can become favored hunting companions and powerful war mounts.

Moorbounders hunt with elongated tusks and retractable claws. With a vague resemblance to large hunting cats, their muscular bodies are built for combat, granting them incredible prowess at leaping and a running speed that makes them dominant carnivores.

Explorer's Guide to Wildemount, pg. 295

    There are few problems with this. When grappling a creature your speed is halved unless the creature you are grappling is two or more sizes smaller than you. So unless you are grappling a creature that is small or tiny your damage in one turn would be 6d6 since you would have 70ft of movement with a dash and a jump takes up 20ft of movement. Only your speed is halved so standing leap would stay the same amount of distance however if it was also halved then you would do 7d6 damage in one turn. Ignoring mechanics for a bit this also seems unfeasible for a moorbounder to do in general or at least as effectively as you described it. The moorbounder would require both forelimbs to grapple something with any sort of effectiveness leaving its back limbs to make the jump alone. Grappling with the jaws might work but anything that is Large or Huge would make jumping difficult or impossible. So from a mechanics standpoint it could be effective, if cheap, tactic against a smaller opponent but I think most DM's would not allow it on anything bigger than medium. Sorry for the essay
