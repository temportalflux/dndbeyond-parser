Necromancer
Medium Humanoid (Any Race), Any Alignment
Armor Class 12 (15 with mage armor)
Hit Points 66 (12d8 + 12)
Speed 30 ft.
STR
9 (-1)
DEX
14 (+2)
CON
12 (+1)
INT
17 (+3)
WIS
12 (+1)
CHA
11 (+0)
Saving Throws INT +7, WIS +5
Skills Arcana +7, History +7
Damage Resistances Necrotic
Senses Passive Perception 11
Languages any four languages
Challenge 9 (5,000 XP)
Proficiency Bonus +4

Spellcasting. The necromancer is a 12th-level spellcaster. Its spellcasting ability is Intelligence (spell save DC 15, +7 to hit with spell attacks). The necromancer has the following wizard spells prepared:

Cantrips (at will): chill touch, dancing lights, mage hand, mending

1st level (4 slots): false life,* mage armor, ray of sickness*

2nd level (3 slots): blindness/deafness,* ray of enfeeblement,* web

3rd level (3 slots): animate dead,* bestow curse,* vampiric touch*

4th level (3 slots): blight,* dimension door, stoneskin

5th level (2 slots): Bigby‚Äôs hand, cloudkill

6th level (1 slot): circle of death*

*Necromancy spell of 1st level or higher

Grim Harvest (1/Turn). When necromancer kills a creature that is neither a construct nor undead with a spell of 1st level or higher, the necromancer regains hit points equal to twice the spell‚Äôs level, or three times if it is a necromancy spell.

Actions

Withering Touch. Melee Spell Attack: +7 to hit, reach 5 ft., one creature. Hit: 5 (2d4) necrotic damage.

Necromancers are specialist wizards who study the interaction of life, death, and undeath. Some like to dig up corpses to create undead slaves. A few use their powers for good, becoming hunters of the undead and risking their lives to save others.

Monster Tags: NPC

Environment: DesertUrban

Volo's Guide to Monsters, pg. 217

    I can get that mindset, however to the best of my understanding of CR as presented in the dungeon master's guide, a monster's CR is meant to judge the relative power of a single monster. Tactics are only taken into consideration as far as "can the monster fly and is fighting players of lv 10 or less?" and "does it know greater invisibility?" tactics like using brutes to guard them and such are taken into consideration at the encounter challenge calculation stage, not the monster's CR calculation. There are some odd examples, like how the 4 genies seem to calculate their CR assuming they have the elemental that they can summon with them (I did the math and the CR works if you add the damage their elemental does to their round to round damage). 

    Also, with regards to how much of its spells it will use, there is consistent language that only the first 3 rounds of combat matter towards a monster's CR. Here is a quote from the DMG for monster creation, under "Overall Damage Output":

    "If a monster‚Äôs damage output varies from round to round, calculate its damage output each round for the first three rounds of combat, and take the average. For example, a young white dragon has a multiattack routine (one bite attack and two claw attacks) that deals an average of 37 damage each round, as well as a breath weapon that deals 45 damage, or 90 if it hits two targets (and it probably will). In the first three rounds of combat, the dragon will probably get to use its breath weapon once and its multiattack routine twice, so its average damage output for the first three rounds would be (90 + 37 + 37) √∑ 3, or 54 damage (rounded down)."

     

    For example, if you reverse engineer the mage's stat block, it assumes they are under the effect of fly or invisibility, and that their first three rounds are cone of cold and then 2 4th level fireballs. It doesn't keep calculating assuming they use more 3rd level fireballs on round 4 and on, as at that point the players should have dealt with the mage or face the reality of having not done so.

    This is not meant to be angry, just trying to get a grasp on stuff like this. I agree that the archmage is too high a CR, as it should have spells like chain lightning or such to be the CR it is. However, this feels like the designers are saying "oh yikes, we don't want DMs to use these NPCs too much, so we'll say they are much higher CR than our math says so that they don't TPK". if they are doing that, i wish they would actually explain themselves somewhere. It's like how the werewolf isn't really CR 3, it's just that the immunity to non-silvered/magical attacks boosts it's HP a lot, and then the designers likely added another CR to account for the abstract idea of "making a PC cursed with lycanthropy"

    Clarification to anybody that reads this thread. Calculating damage output for the purposes of CR assume that targets will fail their saving throws and thus their spells will do the full listed damage. To calculate the number of enemies captured in an Area of Effect, I'm using the section "Targets in Areas of Effect" in the DMG, page 249. Specifically, it states that Sphere or Circle areas hit a number of targets equal to the radius/5. We're assuming a party of 4 as that's the recommended party size. Finally, the assumption for Offensive CR is that the creature will use its most powerful attacks.

    Round 1: Necromancer casts Circle of Death (8d6 damage, 60-foot sphere). 8 x 3.5 = 28 damage per target. Estimated number of targets is 60/5, with a maximum of 4 in our case, so 112 damage.

    Round 2: Necromancer casts Cloudkill (5d8 damage, 20-foot sphere). 5 x 4.5 = 18 damage per target. Estimated number of targets is 20/5, so again 4. 18 x 4 = 72.

    Round 3: Cloudkill is a concentration spell with a duration. Thus it is assumed to persist for more than one round. We can repeat the formula from the previous round to reach 72.

    Now, as per the DMG, we look to find the average across the 3 rounds to determine damage output. 112 + 72 + 72 = 256 / 3 rounds = 85.3 which we average to 85.

    Referring to the CR chart on page 274 of the DMG, we see that this gives us an Offensive CR of 13. The Necromancer's Attack Bonus and Save DC are within 2 points of the CR 13 metric and thus do not affect it.

    Now we can look at the Defensive CR. Referring back to the CR chart, we see that 66 HP lands the Necromancer squarely in the range of CR 1/2 (lol...). Its AC is 2 points higher than the CR 1/2 recommendation placing it up to a much more respectable CR 1.

    Determining the final challenge rating, we take the average CR, 13 + 1 / 2 = 7.

    Wait... CR 7? WTF? Hmm, according to the formula, in order to be CR 9 the Necromancer would need 131-145 HP.

    e: I just tried calculating it assuming the Necromancer is using Stoneskin which would render its effective HP to 132 (even though this isn't RAW, it could have been a slip-up) but because Stoneskin requires concentration the Necromancer couldn't cast Cloudkill. So I replaced it with two castings of Blight, dropping its round-to-round damage quite a bit. The result is still CR 7.
